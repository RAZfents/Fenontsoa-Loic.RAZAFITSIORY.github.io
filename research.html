Measuring Altruism: Precision vs. Simplicity (With Sabine Erica Kröger and Arnaud Dellis)

ABSTRACT:

Social preferences such as trust, fairness, reciprocity, and altruism play a decisive role in economic decision‑making. High levels of trust promote economic growth (Zak & Knack, 2001), while fairness and reciprocity help explain labor‑market phenomena such as wage rigidity or above‑equilibrium wages (Fehr et al., 2009). These preferences also influence contract design, hiring mechanisms, health behaviors (Gaynor et al., 2023), and tax compliance (Hashimzade et al., 2013; Aronsson & Johansson‑Stenman, 2020). Given their broad relevance, failing to incorporate social preferences—especially altruism—into predictive models may generate substantial biases. Reliable and scalable tools for measuring altruism are therefore essential. Two main approaches are used in economics. The first relies on surveys, such as the Preference Values Survey (PVS) (Falk et al., 2016), which uses simple items and a basic dictator game to capture altruistic tendencies. Although easy to implement in large representative samples, this method cannot distinguish different motives driving altruism, such as egalitarianism versus social efficiency. The second approach involves controlled laboratory experiments. The Andreoni and Miller (AM) method employs eight dictator games with varying budgets and price ratios to identify multiple altruistic motives. While theoretically robust, these experiments are time‑intensive and difficult to deploy in representative survey samples requiring lengthy or complex questionnaires.

A more recent contribution by Dellis and Kröger (DK) seeks to bridge these approaches. Their simplified protocol uses three dictator games with a fixed budget and varied transaction costs, making it suitable for large‑scale surveys while still aiming to capture heterogeneity in altruistic motives. However, DK has not yet been rigorously validated, limiting our ability to compare its performance to PVS or AM.

My research examines how PVS, AM, and DK compare in their capacity to identify multidimensional altruism while remaining feasible for inclusion in general population surveys. The project unfolds in two stages. First, the robustness of each method is assessed using the Bronars (1987) test, which evaluates an experimental design’s sensitivity to violations of the Generalized Axiom of Revealed Preference (GARP) by random decision‑makers. Existing evidence shows that AM has high statistical power (78.1%), whereas PVS performs poorly (0%), and DK performs modestly (11%).

We then search for improvements to DK. Using simulations and geometric analysis inspired by Bronars (1987) and Aizcorbe (1991), we examine alternative budget‑constraint configurations and identify those that maximize test power. A more powerful design better distinguishes rational from random behavior, enabling consistent estimation of quasi‑concave utility functions when choices satisfy GARP (Varian, 1992). This improved version is referred to as DKR.

Second, we compare the three methods, PVS, AM, and DKR, in an experiment with crossover design, in which all participants completed each method in randomized order. The objective is to evaluate their relative ability to detect different types of altruism and to determine whether DKR provides a reliable yet simple multidimensional measure. If validated, DKR could offer a practical tool for large surveys and support new research in several areas of economics, including public finance, labor economics, and health economics.
